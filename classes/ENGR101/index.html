Frameworks and Sources

November 11, 2019

To facilitate independent consideration of the dilemma presented and to minimize groupthink, we split up for a day to write out how we applied our frameworks to the issue at hand. These four write-ups are below:

MatthewD:

I normally take a pragmatic point of view on ethical issues. However, here, since most affects can be accounted for in terms of long-term ramifications, pragmatism decays to utilitarianism. Thus, utilitarianism is the view I use.

From this, I actually disagree with the importance ranking of the previous post. As a starting point, I believe a minimum level of environmental protection should be first and foremost, as not turning our planet into a hothouse wasteland is critical to the survival of our species. Thus, I see the removal of the disposable printer ink cartridges as a significant boon to the environment first, my personal wealth and welfare responsibility second, and as my corporate duty third.

However, I agree with the economic status of my hometown being a last-place concern, as I believe market demand will continue for the disposable cartridges for some time. The new generation of printers will not roll out instantaneously, and so neither will the demand for the disposable cartridges disappear instantly. This will give the families of my hometown time to make plans for other employment or possibly plans to move to a different area where employment is more plentiful.

In utility, here, there is a significant gain for the world from this new development, combined with a minimal cost in relocating maybe a few thousand people, as dictated by market forces. I believe it would be worse for them to cling to the past, continuing to believe those disposable cartridge manufacturing jobs would last forever.

Finally, from the perspective under John Rawls’ Veil of Ignorance, this is (over the long term) an improvement in environmental conditions at medium, non-life-threatening, finite cost to a miniscule part of overall society. This is, I believe, an acceptable decision from that perspective.

Kerry:

Based on my ethical framework of pragmatism and utilitarianism, I would go with the Utilitarian Approach. I think moving on to the plan of the refillable cartridges can eventually benefit more people. Despite the shift from the current disposable ones might bring short-term financial crisis and unemployment problems to the local people, it allows our future generations to live in a beautiful Twin Peaks and can result in a more sustainable environment as a whole.

To resolve the temporary concerns of the locals, we can announce the layoffs in advance so they can make moving plans accordingly. The refillable cartridges will also cut our profits significantly. However, since the demand of refilling cartridges will increase, we can downsize our manufacturing plant and construct some cartridge refill stations and introduce new positions to help with the refilling process to fill the profit gap. This can be brought out slowly, as the current disposable cartridges can still last five to seven years.

Zach:

While our decision could help reduce the waste problem for the environment, the highest priority for the company should be to maximize profits. Yes we will not supply as many products that use disposable ink cartridges, but our decision should not be swayed by its environmental pros. Rather, it should be in response to the changing market’s new awareness for the harmfulness of disposable ink cartridges. The decision’s beneficial implications for the environment shall be recognized as a bonus (and could possibly be used in advertisements).

As for the residents of Twin Peaks, their labor will still be needed as long as our products that use disposable ink cartridges are still being sold. On the surface, this decision gives off the illusion of a false dichotomy between extinguishing the Twin Peaks economy or not adapting to market demands. We can still supply older printer models, while advertising our newer more environmentally-friendly models, so our company can look ethical to the public.

My framework for ethical thinking is a mix between pragmatism and utilitarianism as my aim is to bring as much success as possible to the company. In doing so, we can find a viable way to maximize employment in the county of Twin Peaks, and we can advertise our company as being progressive. The potential drop in employment at the plant is a consequence that Twin Peaks will have to endure.

Chen:

The decision of BLI replacing disposable printers ink cartridges with refillable one brings up an ethical dilemma here: Such a move would be helpful for reducing the amount of disposed wastes which also improves environmental conditions, however, making residents of Twin Peaks losing their job opportunities due to reduced cartridges production. It seems like the regular way to resolve this dilemma is to sacrifice one aspect and so only the other gets benefited. However, it’s actually unnecessary to satisfy one and sacrifice another under the ethical concept of eclecticism: There is still a middle ground where the interests of both employees and environments can stand, and eclecticism is aimed to discover such place by making rational trade offs. The final move does not have to prefer one over another when conflicting interests exist by employing eclecticism in such kinds of dilemma.

Finally, we look at brief overviews of how each ethical framework covered in the first class lecture would make their decisions on this topic:

Utilitarianism:

Seeking to do the greatest good for the greatest number, in this case, would require that we protect the environment and develop the refillable cartridges. Our hometown is a small number of people, whereas negative environmental effects will negatively affect all people who exist.

Deontological/Duty Ethics:

Applying the Kantian “Universality Principle,” we see that, if every employee on the planet worked against their corporate interests in favor of their hometown, then capitalism would collapse, work on exported goods such as food would not be completed, and millions would starve. This is an unacceptable outcome, so it must be immoral to act against our corporate duty. Therefore, we must complete the project to develop the refillable printer cartridges.

Rights Ethics:

Developing the refillable cartridges violates the right to employment of the people of our hometown, without providing any solution. This is an unacceptable violation of the rights of our hometown, so we cannot assist BLI in perpetrating it. We must not develop refillable printer cartridges.

Virtue Ethics/Stoicism:

To quote the lecture slide on virtue ethics, “Not much guidance in terms of proper actions.” At best, we could look at the conflict between our virtue of Loyalty (to our hometown) versus the virtues of Order, Duty, and Honor demanded by BLI. But this then becomes a question of which virtues we value more. The decision is binary; the sweet spot aimed for by virtue ethics does not exist. This framework leaves the decision to us, only telling us to suck it up through the consequences.

Pragmatism:

What practical difference exists between producing the new, refillable generation, and not?

  • Producing the new generation leads to an eventual shutdown of the plant in our hometown after some years of demand decline, our continued employment at BLI, the continued existence of BLI and a reduction in environmental damage compared to a disposable-only future.
  • Refusing to complete the project leads to myself being fired, and possibly my research team. BLI’s competitors fill the void in demand for refillable cartridges. Then, after a similar period of demand decline, our hometown’s plant is shut down. The environmental damage reduction is the same.

The differences are the end of our employment and the possible shutdown of BLI. As we prefer a world where we are employed, we should complete our project and produce the refillable cartridge printer generation.

Eclecticism:

It is not originated from a single ethical concept but rather making use of many frameworks or paradigms as a part of the decision making process. In this case, BLI can consider transforming the factory in Twin Peaks into a refilling station or printer service center: Residents of Twin Peaks would possibly not producing cartridges, but they can instead help customers with their printer issues including refilling cartridges. This makes sure that Twin Peaks avoid unemployment crisis while environment is also put into consideration.

In considering these frameworks, we investigated a number of sources. First and foremost were the class lecture slides’ descriptions, our notes, and Robert Barger’s “Can we find a single Ethical Code?” article.

We also discovered that, under this dilemma, pragmatism and utilitarianism decay to nearly identical arguments, and so we spent some time investigating the difference between the two using results such as https://philosophy.stackexchange.com/questions/199/what-is-a-straight-forward-method-for-distinguishing-pragmatic-from-utilitarian. This led to the realization that, while utilitarians focus on only the amount of human happiness and suffering, pragmatists, identify the differences between the worlds, taking into account more factors than just happiness and/or harm. (Like, say, corporate profits.)

We also took into account the frameworks we expressed in our midterms in building our views on this dilemma. As our midterms showed clearly our personal frameworks and their grounding, they were valuable tools for translating our feelings about the current problem into ethical and moral arguments.

Stakeholders and Externalities

November 10, 2019

In considering the ethical dilemma reiterated in our previous blog post, we face the question of who our decision may affect. There is, present in our decision, both a short-term impact on people directly involved in our project and its result, and a possible long-term impact on larger systems the world over.

Our company is transforming our product to be better for the environment as demanded by the market. Here are the direct, short-term stakeholders impacted by the results:

  • Black Lodge Industries -- as the commissioner of the project, and directly benefiting from any discoveries thereof, BLI is an obvious stakeholder.
  • Ourselves and our research team -- Face risks from our employers that we might lose our jobs if any individual demonstrates dissent towards developing refillable cartridges.
  • BLI Customers -- because refillable cartridges are likely to be more complex and possibly harder to replace, negatively impacting user experience.
  • Employment of the factory workers in our hometown -- Local economy may suffer when the production of cartridges comes to an end. Residents of Twin Peaks would undergo unemployment which would have major impact on their daily life.

We are also excited for what our new product may potentially bring about for everybody overall, around the globe:

  • The Environment -- Continued use of disposable cartridges will lead to serious soil pollution when left in landfill. Refilling cartridges, on the other hand, can conserve energy and natural resources.
  • The printer market -- moving on to the reusable cartridges can restore market share since the competitors have developed such products on the printer markets.
  • The Human impact -- improving the environmental awareness of the public, convincing people to consume additional green products. And if those products come from BLI, the more the better!

Now, given the actual impacts imposed on each of these parties and systems, we consider our stakeholders in a particular order of importance to our ethical decision making:

  1. BLI
  2. Printer market customers
  3. Our own jobs
  4. Environment
  5. Twin Peaks Economy

We consider BLI’s interest first and foremost, as they were a valuable employer to us in a time of economic slowdown. We feel that helping the company’s success is likely to lead to additional jobs for many other people. As a second consideration, the market is currently pushing for environmental concerns, and obeying the sway of the market is a reasonable good as it makes many people happier with BLI. Third, should we choose to refuse to work on the refillable cartridge project, we would lose our jobs, bringing upon ourselves significant economic hardship -- and possibly bringing that upon the research team below us as well. Our environmentalism, ignored in the past by BLI, actually is in line with their decision-making process in this case, and with the first three points.

Only last do we consider the economy of our hometown, Twin Peaks. As the disposable cartridge printers are likely to exist on the market for some time, there will still be a period during which the company can warn of future layoffs and restructuring associated with the reusable cartridge program. With this proper warning, residents of Twin Peaks can plan for their financial futures. Though it may require moving away, it is unlikely that any of them will face an unavoidable financial crisis.

The Ethical Dilemma Ahead

November 8, 2019

Our team, Iron Giants, has been tasked with determining an ethical pathway through the following dilemma:

45% of our hometown’s economy relies on Inkjet Printer cartridges, an environmentally unsustainable technology providing the cheapest high-resolution printing capabilities on the market. The cartridges could be made refillable rather than completely disposable, but the company has elected for the cheaper disposable only approach. This leads to a significant environmental concern, but one we have ignored in the past due to the economic support its industry provides.

We have been placed in charge of an R&D team developing a new generation of inkjet printers that do not use disposable cartridges. We are concerned because this may lead to downsizing or shutdown of the cartridge plant in our hometown, stunting our hometown’s economy and placing many residents in dire financial situations. There exists also an environmental concern posed by continuing to produce disposable cartridges.

The dilemma presented is the torn concerns between supporting our hometown, or supporting the environment and our corporate duty as engineers.

We are also posed two evolutions of the scenario:

Scenario 2

Some members of our research team are not convinced of the environmental impacts caused by disposable ink cartridges. However, the market is very aware that this is a big problem. A good research team should always consider the demands of the market.

Scenario 3

As our development and research team moves on to a proposal of non-disposable and refillable ink cartridges, it will put more people in unemployment status because of the decrease in the demand of producing cartridges. That raises the ethical dilemma of whether we need to be concerned about the unemployment issue in our hometown while designing the printers for the next generation.
